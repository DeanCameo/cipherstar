The Caesar Cipher, named after Julius Caesar, is a simple substitution encryption technique. Each letter in the plaintext is shifted by a fixed number of positions down the alphabet. It's a basic and easily understandable form of encryption but lacks security for modern applications.

The Substitution Cipher is a broader category of encryption where each letter in the plaintext is replaced with another letter or symbol according to a predefined substitution key. Unlike Caesar Cipher, the substitution key can be more complex, offering greater variability in encoding.

The Vigenère Cipher is a method of encrypting text using a simple form of polyalphabetic substitution. Instead of a single shift Vigenère uses a keyword to determine multiple shift values. The keyword is repeated to match the length of the plaintext, creating a more complex and secure encryption.
